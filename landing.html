Exploring multi-planetary systems

Keywords: Planetary systems – Planets and satellites: detection – Planets and satellites: formation and evolution – Planets and satellites: characterization

Background

To truly identify our place in this universe, knowledge of other planetary systems – ‘what lies beyond’ – is necessary. The field of exoplanets has continued to grow since and its appearance in common media is dominated by artists illustrations of strange worlds like an entire planet of diamond composition (Madhusudhan (2012)), a planet with iron rain (Ehrenreich et al. (2020)), a planet casting shadows on two stars (Kostov et al. (2021)) and so on. It is clear that the scientific community has discovered a staggering diversity in the nature of exoplanets (Armstrong et al. (2020), Winn, Sanchis-Ojeda, and Rappaport (2018), Santerne et al. (2019), Udry and Santos (2007)). An analysis of exoplanet detections reveals the existence of diversity, not only in exoplanets but in planetary systems found as well (Lissauer et al. (2011), Fabrycky et al. (2014), Winn and Fabrycky (2015)). This enables us to constrain planet formation and evolution theories better, given that the only available reference to test these theories earlier had been the solar system. It also puts the solar system in question: is it a commonly found configuration or is it one of a kind? To tackle such questions regarding planet formation, a statistical analysis of multi-planetary systems becomes essential (Lissauer et al. (2011), Fabrycky et al. (2014), Rowe et al. (2015)).

About

Multi-planetary systems are often found bearing striking resemblance to the solar system, exhibiting features like quasi-circular and quasi-coplanar orbits. The eccentricities and mutual inclinations generally decrease with the observed multiplicity (number of planets in a system) of a planetary system, and several observed single-planetary systems are found to lie on highly eccentric orbits. It is yet unclear if there exists a smooth correlation between the eccentricity and multiplicity, and the inclination and multiplicity. In this work, the existence of these correlations is investigated by studying the orbital architectures of theoretically simulated planetary systems and comparing them with observations. A novel architecture classification framework presented in Mishra et al. 2022 (in review, submitted to A&A) is applied to the theoretically simulated and theoretically observed planetary systems to enable further systematic studies of the arrangement and distribution of planetary quantities like the eccentricity and inclination, within a single planetary system. Since the eccentricity and mutual inclination of planets in a system are remnants of planet formation and evolution histories, this work provides insights on planet formation and evolution processes, uniquely aided by classifying architecture.

Methods

KOBE, a computer code introduced in Mishra et al. (2021), was further developed to enable the study of transit duration statistics. The code was modified in several ways to mimic observational surveys with increased accuracy. The assumption of circular orbits was removed, better approximations for the transit durations were set in using inputs from Kipping (2010) and Winn (2010). A monte-carlo approach was set up to estimate the number of transits and a better approximation for the transit signal was introduced. The Kepler pipeline detection efficiency was simulated following Christiansen (2017) and the duty cycle was added to the calculations. The updated KOBE was then used to simulate the geometrical limitations of the transit method and apply the detection biases and completeness of the Kepler survey on a synthetic population of planets. Synthetic planetary systems were simulated using the Generation III Bern Model. This allowed simulated planetary systems to be compared with observations to gain insights on the eccentricities and inclinations in planetary systems. The simulated and theoretically observed populations were classified based on their mass architectures and their orbital architectures are studied.
